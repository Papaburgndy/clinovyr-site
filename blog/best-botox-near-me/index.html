Who Is Qualified to Inject Botox?

This is the most important thing to get right before anything else. Botox (botulinum toxin) is a prescription medication, and in virtually every US state, it must be prescribed and administered by or under the direct supervision of a licensed medical professional.

✓ Qualified Injectors

  • MD / DO (physician)
  • Nurse Practitioner (NP)
  • Physician Assistant (PA)
  • Registered Nurse (RN) — under physician supervision
  • Dentist (for certain facial areas)

✗ Not Qualified

  • Licensed aesthetician or esthetician
  • "Certified injector" with no medical license
  • Someone who took a weekend course
  • Cosmetologist
  • Spa staff without medical credentials
Important: Some states allow RNs or PAs to inject independently if a physician is "available" by phone. Always ask: "Are you a licensed NP, PA, or MD?" — not just "Are you certified?" Anyone can claim a "certification."

How Much Does Botox Cost in 2026?

Botox pricing comes in two models: per unit or per area. Understanding the difference protects you from overpaying.

Per Unit vs. Per Area Pricing

Per unit pricing is more transparent. You can verify how many units you received and compare the per-unit cost. The standard price is $12–$20 per unit at most reputable practices.

Per area pricing bundles the cost but hides how many units you're actually getting. A provider who charges $250 "per area" may give you 10 units; another gives 20. Always ask how many units are included in the quoted price.

How to Find the Best Botox Provider Near You

The right provider makes the difference between natural, beautiful results and the "frozen" or asymmetrical look that people are afraid of. Here's exactly what to look for:

The 5 Things That Actually Matter

  • Medical credentials verified: Confirm their license on your state's medical board website. Takes 60 seconds at your state's medical board website. Non-negotiable.
  • Portfolio of real patients: Ask to see before/after photos from patients treated at this specific practice. Insist on actual photos — not stock imagery or images from manufacturer training materials.
  • Injection technique discussion: A great injector will ask about your goals, assess your facial anatomy, and explain exactly what areas and units they're recommending before picking up a needle. If they quote a price without asking about your goals, walk out.
  • Transparent unit pricing: Ask: "How many units will you use, and what is the per-unit price?" If they refuse to answer or only give "per area" pricing without disclosing units, that's a red flag.
  • Follows up: Top providers include a 2-week follow-up (in person or by photo) to assess results and offer a complimentary touch-up if needed. This costs the provider nothing significant but shows they stand behind their work.

5 Red Flags When Choosing a Botox Provider

  • They can't tell you their medical license type or you can't verify it
  • Price is below $10/unit or they're offering "today only" flash sales
  • They recommend the maximum number of units at every consultation
  • No consultation — they go straight to injection without asking about your goals or history
  • Reviews mention asymmetry, drooping (ptosis), or results lasting only 6–8 weeks

How Long Does Botox Last?

For most first-time patients, Botox lasts 3–4 months. With regular treatments (every 3–4 months), many patients find their results extend to 4–5 months over time, because the treated muscles weaken slightly from consistent use.

Factors that reduce longevity:

  • High metabolism (frequent intense exercise)
  • Too few units for the area treated
  • Certain areas (lip flip and brow lift metabolize faster than glabella)
  • First-time treatment — the first round often lasts slightly less

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does Botox cost in 2026?

Botox is priced at $12–$20 per unit at most reputable practices. A typical 3-area treatment (forehead, 11s, crow's feet) uses 50–80 units and costs $700–$1,500 depending on the city and provider type. Avoid pricing below $9/unit — it's a warning sign for counterfeit product or extreme underdosing.

Who is qualified to inject Botox?

In most states, Botox must be prescribed and administered by or under the supervision of a licensed MD, DO, NP, or PA. Aestheticians and non-medical "certified injectors" are not legally qualified to inject Botox regardless of what certifications they claim.

How long does Botox last?

Botox typically lasts 3–4 months for first-time patients and can extend to 4–6 months with regular treatments over time. Results vary by area — the glabella (11s) tends to last longest, while the lip flip metabolizes fastest.

What is the difference between Botox and Dysport?

Both are botulinum toxin type A, but Dysport spreads more (better for forehead, risky near eyes), kicks in faster (2–3 days vs. 5–7), and is cheaper per unit but requires 2.5–3x more units. Many injectors prefer one over the other based on training and the area being treated.

What should I look for in a Botox provider?

Medical license verification, a portfolio of real patients (not stock photos), a proper consultation before any injection, per-unit transparent pricing, and a follow-up policy. Natural-looking results come from skilled technique and appropriate dosing — not from the brand of product used.

Is cheap Botox safe?

Botox under $9–$10/unit or "Botox parties" at non-medical venues are serious red flags. Risks include counterfeit product, extreme underdosing, and unqualified injectors. The sweet spot for reputable care is $12–$18/unit in most US cities.
