✅ The 7-Question Med Spa Vetting Checklist

Use this before booking at any med spa. A reputable clinic will have clear, confident answers to every single one of these questions.

🔍 The Clinovyr Med Spa Vetting Checklist (2026)
✓
1. Who is the medical director and what are their credentials?
A legitimate med spa must have a licensed physician (MD or DO) as medical director. Ask for their name and look them up on your state's medical board website (takes 2 minutes). If they can't or won't give you a name, leave.
✓
2. Who will physically be performing my treatment?
In most states, Botox and filler injections must be performed by or directly supervised by a licensed medical professional — typically RN, NP, PA, or MD. Ask to see their state license number. Estheticians alone cannot legally perform injections in most states.
✓
3. Is the facility licensed as a medical spa in your state?
Med spa licensing requirements vary by state. In California, Florida, Texas, and New York, med spas must hold specific facility licenses. Ask to see the facility license — it should be posted prominently alongside practitioner licenses.
✓
4. What injectable brands do you use?
For Botox: ask whether they use Botox (Allergan), Dysport (Galderma), or Xeomin (Merz) — all FDA-approved brands. For fillers: Juvederm, Restylane, and Sculptra are the gold standards. Vague answers or unfamiliar brand names are red flags.
✓
5. Can you show me before-and-after photos of your actual patients?
Stock photos or influencer posts don't count. Ask to see real patient results for your specific treatment. A confident, experienced injector will have a portfolio. Someone who deflects this question likely doesn't have good results to show.
✓
6. What is your protocol for managing complications?
Every procedure carries some risk. A professional clinic has written protocols for managing adverse events — including hyaluronidase on-site for filler complications. If the answer is "it won't happen" rather than "here's what we do if it does," that's a serious warning sign.
✓
7. Are you offering an in-person consultation before treatment?
Any reputable med spa requires a consultation before injections or laser treatments. If they're willing to book you for Botox without ever evaluating your facial anatomy or medical history, walk away.

🚩 Red Flags: Walk Away from Any Med Spa That Does These Things

The fastest way to spot an unsafe med spa isn't a checklist — it's knowing what dealbreakers look like in practice.

⚠️ Immediate Red Flags — Do Not Proceed

  • Cannot name the medical director — legally required in almost every state
  • Offers extreme discounts on injectables (e.g., "$6/unit Botox") — likely diluted or counterfeit product
  • Practitioner avoids discussing credentials or gets defensive when asked
  • No consent form or medical history intake before treatment
  • Will treat you without a consultation — pressures immediate booking
  • Cannot show before-and-after photos from their own patients
  • No hyaluronidase on site for filler emergency reversal
  • Performs injections in non-clinical spaces (pop-up events, hotel rooms, etc.)
  • Pressure to sign up for packages before you've had a treatment
  • Unlicensed "beauty injectors" or "aestheticians" doing injectables without RN/NP/PA/MD credentials

👩‍⚕️ Credentials: Who Should Be Doing What

One of the biggest sources of confusion for patients is understanding which practitioners are legally and clinically qualified to perform which treatments.

✅ Qualified Injectors

  • MD (Medical Doctor) — all treatments
  • DO (Doctor of Osteopathic Medicine) — all treatments
  • NP (Nurse Practitioner) — with prescriptive authority
  • PA (Physician Assistant) — under MD supervision
  • RN (Registered Nurse) — under MD supervision in most states
  • CRNA — under appropriate supervision
  • DMD/DDS (for perioral treatments in some states)

❌ Not Qualified for Injections

  • Licensed Esthetician (LE) — cannot inject
  • Cosmetologist — cannot inject
  • "Beauty injector" (unlicensed title)
  • LPN (Limited Practical Nurse) — cannot inject in most states
  • Massage Therapist — cannot inject
  • Medical Assistant (MA) — cannot inject
  • Anyone without a state-issued prescriptive or clinical license

💆 Most Popular Med Spa Treatments in 2026

The aesthetic medicine landscape has expanded significantly. Here's what patients are actually booking in 2026:

1. Botox & Neuromodulators

Still the #1 med spa treatment by volume. Botox (Allergan), Dysport (Galderma), Xeomin (Merz), and Daxxify (Revance) all target the same neuromuscular junction but differ in onset, duration, and spread. Average treatment: 20–50 units for forehead + crow's feet + glabella. Results last 3–4 months for most patients (Daxxify lasts 6+ months). Cost: $300–$900 per session depending on units used and city.

2. Dermal Fillers

Hyaluronic acid fillers (Juvederm, Restylane) remain the most popular, but bio-stimulators like Sculptra (poly-L-lactic acid) and Radiesse (calcium hydroxyapatite) have seen major growth for volume restoration and collagen stimulation. Filler requires the most anatomical expertise — it should only be performed by injectors with extensive facial anatomy training. Cost: $700–$1,600 per syringe.

3. Body Contouring

CoolSculpting (cryolipolysis) remains popular for stubborn fat pockets. Emsculpt Neo (combined radiofrequency + HIFEM) has seen explosive growth for simultaneous fat reduction and muscle building. These are non-surgical but require proper assessment — not everyone is a good candidate. Cost: $800–$2,000 per treatment cycle.

4. RF Microneedling

Morpheus8, Vivace, and Potenza combine micro-injuries with radiofrequency energy to stimulate collagen and tighten skin. Highly effective for skin laxity, acne scars, and overall texture. These require medical device operator training and should be performed by or directly supervised by a medical professional. Cost: $800–$2,000 per session.

5. Laser Skin Treatments

IPL photofacials, Fraxel, CO2 laser, and pulsed dye lasers address pigmentation, redness, texture, and resurfacing. These vary dramatically in downtime (none for IPL; 1–2 weeks for ablative CO2). Operator training matters enormously — an improperly calibrated CO2 laser causes permanent scarring.

❓ Frequently Asked Questions

How do I find the best med spa near me?
The best way to find a reputable med spa is to verify that a licensed physician (MD or DO) is the medical director, confirm all injectors hold RN, NP, or PA credentials, check that the facility has a state med spa license, read reviews on multiple platforms (Google, RealSelf, Yelp), and ask for before-and-after photos of actual patients. Use Clinovyr to instantly access pre-vetted providers in your area.
What is the difference between a med spa and a regular spa?
A medical spa (med spa) offers clinical aesthetic treatments that require medical training and licensing — such as Botox, dermal fillers, laser treatments, chemical peels, and microneedling. These procedures carry real medical risks and must be performed or directly supervised by licensed medical professionals. A regular day spa offers wellness services like massages and basic facials that do not require medical oversight.
How much does a med spa treatment cost?
Med spa costs vary widely by treatment and city. Botox typically costs $12–$22 per unit ($300–$700 per session). Dermal fillers run $700–$1,600 per syringe. Laser hair removal costs $150–$500 per session depending on body area. CoolSculpting costs $750–$2,000 per cycle. In New York and Los Angeles, prices run 20–40% higher than the national average.
Are med spas safe?
Med spas are safe when treatments are performed by properly credentialed practitioners under physician supervision. Risks increase significantly at facilities that allow unlicensed staff to perform injections or laser treatments. Always verify practitioner credentials through your state medical board or nursing board before any treatment.
What questions should I ask at a med spa consultation?
Ask: (1) Who is the medical director and what are their credentials? (2) Who will be performing my treatment and what is their license number? (3) How many times have you performed this specific procedure? (4) What are the risks and how do you handle complications? (5) What FDA-approved product brands do you use? (6) Can I see before-and-after photos of your actual patients?
What's the best med spa treatment for anti-aging?
The most effective anti-aging combination is Botox or Dysport for dynamic wrinkles (forehead, crow's feet), hyaluronic acid fillers for volume loss (cheeks, under-eyes, lips), and either RF microneedling or laser resurfacing for skin texture and tone. Bio-stimulators like Sculptra work well for longer-term volume and collagen stimulation. A personalized treatment plan from a board-certified dermatologist or plastic surgeon will produce the best outcomes.
How do I know if a med spa is legitimate?
Legitimate med spas will: have a licensed physician as medical director (verify on your state medical board website), display all practitioner licenses, provide written treatment plans and consent forms, use FDA-approved devices and products, maintain clinical-grade sterilization, and have protocols for managing adverse events. Red flags include extreme discounts, pressure to purchase packages before treatment, practitioners who deflect credential questions, and facilities that feel more like retail stores than medical offices.
